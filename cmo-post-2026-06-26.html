Good judgement has nothing to do with fewer mistakes. It took me years to understand that. Every bad call I ever made, I made the same way. Gathered more information. Asked more people. Waited for certainty that was never coming. Then moved too late, or not at all. The problem isn't a shortage of data. Most of the time you have enough to decide. The real problem is that you're waiting to feel certain before you act. Real judgement is something different. It's the ability to read what you actually know, separate it from what you wish were true, and commit anyway. Not confidently. Just clearly. I teach people to fly paragliders. The go/no-go call before a flight is rarely obvious. The wind can look fine. The cloud base can look high enough. But something is off. Learning to trust that read, and act on it before your brain rationalises you out of it, is a skill. It takes repetition. And it requires you to get it wrong sometimes, notice what that felt like, and adjust. You don't develop judgement by avoiding mistakes. You develop it by making decisions faster than the situation changes, then paying close attention to what happened. Do that 50 times and you'll call it better than any framework you've ever read. Good luck. P.S. The best decision-makers I know are wrong a lot. They just recover quickly.

Option 1 · Extend
The other thing worth saying: the feedback loop matters as much as the decision. A call you made 6 months ago and never revisited teaches you nothing. The reps only count if you go back and look at what actually happened versus what you expected. That's where the calibration lives.
Option 2 · Humanise
I've called it wrong in the air before. Committed to a flight when I should have walked. Nothing dramatic happened. But I knew on the drive home. That feeling is the data. You don't get access to it by reading about it.
Option 3 · CTA amplifier
If this landed, the next step is simple. Pick one decision you've been sitting on for more than a week. Not a life decision. A real, specific, low-stakes call. Make it today. Write down what you expected. Check it in 30 days. That's the first rep.
