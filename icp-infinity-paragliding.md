## SECTION 3: THE CORE PROBLEM THEY WANT SOLVED

**Problem 1:** They want to fly freely — but they don't know how to do it safely and with confidence.
The desire for freedom is real. The knowledge, skill, and judgement to enter the sport independently is not yet there.

**Problem 2:** They can feel the gap between wanting to fly and knowing they're not yet equipped to do it well.
The ambition exists. The pathway to autonomous, safe flight does not — until now.

**Problem 3 (returning international pilots):** They've trained elsewhere and arrive realising how little they actually learned. Their previous school didn't hold them to a standard high enough to fly with real confidence and sound judgement.

**Why they haven't solved it yet:**
Most students arrive brand new — with no frame of reference for what high-quality instruction looks like. Returning international pilots who arrive with a licence frequently discover the gap: they had a piece of paper but not the decision-making depth behind it. What was missing in their prior training was a higher standard of expectation and genuine pilotage accountability.

---

## SECTION 4: WHAT THEY WANT INSTEAD

**Tangible outcome:** An IPPI international pilot licence earned through genuine competence — with skills, log book hours, and flight ability that reflect real capability.

**Emotional outcome:** Confidence. The ability to stand on a launch site, read the conditions, and know with certainty whether today is a day to fly.

**Identity outcome:** They want to become a pilot — not someone who did a paragliding course. The shift is from student or passenger to autonomous decision-maker who takes full responsibility for their flying.

---

## SECTION 5: WHAT FRUSTRATES THEM ABOUT OTHER OPTIONS

| Frustration | Why It Matters |
|-------------|----------------|
| Low training standards elsewhere | Students leave with a licence but without the judgement to fly safely — producing dangerous pilots and short-lived flying careers |
| Being treated as a flight-count statistic | Schools focused on numbers rather than pilot quality leave students under-confident and underprepared |
| No accountability for decision-making | Without learning to own their go/no-go calls, pilots become dependent rather than autonomous |
| Aviation reduced to a sport like skiing | Minimising the real stakes of paragliding leads to poor judgement, poor outcomes, and pilots who leave the sport early or worse |

## SECTION 7: ICP PERSONA SUMMARY

The ideal Infinity Paragliding student is a growth-minded 25–40-year-old who sees paragliding not as a bucket-list activity but as a sport they intend to pursue for years. They want the freedom of autonomous flight — and they know, honestly, that they don't yet have the skills or judgement to get there safely. They've looked into the sport enough to know they need proper instruction, but may not know what "proper" actually looks like. When they find Infinity — through Google, a review, or a recommendation from someone who trained there — they're not looking for a short course. They want to be built into a real pilot. They respond well to high standards and early responsibility. For the returning international pilot, the turning point is the moment of honest realisation: they held a licence, but not the competence that should come with it. That moment is what makes them fully commit to doing it properly the second time.

## SECTION 8: CONTENT DIRECTION FROM ICP

- **Topics that resonate:** What autonomous pilotage actually means; the difference between a licensed pilot and a capable one; how to read conditions and make go/no-go calls; why flight count is a poor measure of pilot quality; what happens when aviation is not taken seriously
- **Tone that connects:** Direct, authoritative, no-fluff — this audience respects real expertise and responds to plainspoken competence over inspiration or hype
- **Proof they need:** Real student transformation stories (Grant); curriculum depth versus IPPI minimums; 15 years flying, 13 years teaching; SIV instructor qualification; international training with top European SIV schools
- **What to avoid:** "Fun for everyone" messaging; anything that softens aviation's seriousness; framing success by licence or flight count alone; language that positions paragliding as comparable to skiing or mountain biking
