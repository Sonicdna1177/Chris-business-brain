# VOICE DNA PROFILE
## Chris Connolly

---

## SECTION 1: CONVERSATIONAL MARKERS

### Primary fillers and connectors

Chris uses connective language sparingly and purposefully. His dominant connector is **"and"** — used not as padding but to chain consequences in a single breath, building momentum without inflection.

> "It was very dark, very bleak, very lost, very alone."
> "I have become a more confident business person and investor as a result."
> "They learn meteorology to a high level, and from that they can understand good judgement, and from good judgement they can make a clear decision of go or no-go."

He does not use filler words (um, honestly, basically, look). His answers are notably clean of verbal padding. Frequency: rare to none.

### Transition phrases

Chris transitions with structure, not softeners. His transitions are logical, not conversational:

- "When I came out the other side..." (consequence of endurance)
- "What happened on the other side of it?" (self-asked rhetorical to pivot)
- "The cost taught me that..." (consequence framing)
- "Now I think..." (before/after contrast opener)
- "From day one..." (immediacy and positioning opener)

### Sentence openers

1. Direct statement: "They are an autonomous pilot."
2. Time-anchored: "After four years of competing..."
3. Conditional/consequential: "When I found something... / When I came out the other side..."
4. Self-referential declaration: "I used to think... / Now I think..."

## SECTION 2: SENTENCE PATTERNS

### Average length

Short to medium. Most sentences run 10–18 words. He uses short punchy declarations as anchors, then follows with medium explanatory sentences. Rarely exceeds 25 words in a single sentence.

### Preferred structures

**Short punchy declarations (4–8 words):**
> "That hurt badly."
> "They are an autonomous pilot."
> "Failing at the Paragliding World Championships."
> "If you want something, go out and get it."

**Medium explanatory sentences (10–18 words):**
> "Going into financial debt was a real risk."
> "I became the number one student in New Zealand in the first year of study."

**Triadic repetition for emphasis:**
> "It was very dark, very bleak, very lost, very alone."
> "reset, regroup, and reimagine"

**Cause → Effect chains:**
> "They learn meteorology to a high level, and from that they can understand good judgement, and from good judgement they can make a clear decision of go or no-go."

### Sentence variety

Chris naturally alternates between short declarations and longer explanatory chains. The short statements do the punching. The long chains do the teaching. This creates a natural speaking rhythm that's easy to follow.

### Three most common sentence openers

1. "When I..." (temporal, personal)
2. "It was..." (direct scene-setting)
3. "[Subject] [verb]." (declarative — no preamble)

## SECTION 4: EMOTIONAL SIGNATURE

### Primary tone: Measured Conviction

Chris is direct without being aggressive. Confident without being boastful. He states things plainly and moves on — he doesn't oversell a point or circle back to it for reassurance.

> "I believed that to be the right thing at the time and still do."
> "It was one of the best things I've ever done."
> "That hurt badly."

Three words. No elaboration. He trusts the sentence to carry the weight.

### Secondary tone: Reflective Clarity (when discussing failures and turning points)

When the topic shifts to hardship or transformation, Chris slows down and gets precise. He names the feeling without dramatising it.

> "It was very dark, very bleak, very lost, very alone."
> "It also wasn't what I thought it would be."
> "It didn't give me the satisfaction that I wanted it to."

He stays factual even when the content is emotional. He describes the experience without performing it.

### Tonal shifts

Default register: direct and forward-facing. When discussing loss, failure, or cost: measured and honest, never victimised. When discussing his method or philosophy: declarative and certain. When discussing students or outcomes: quietly proud, specific.

### Confidence markers

**When certain:**
> "I knew I'd never go back there..."
> "From day one, we place the entire emphasis on..."
> "I believed that to be the right thing at the time and still do."

He signals certainty through declarative statements — no hedging, no "I think" or "maybe."

**When uncertain or exploring:**
> "I think the industry is going to move more into digital learning..."
> "I used to think that success was determined by..."

He only uses "I think" when explicitly offering a forward-looking view or describing a former belief he has since updated. This makes his "I think" moments more credible — they signal genuine interpretation, not weakness.

## SECTION 6: IDEA FLOW PATTERN

### Dominant pattern: Situation → Cost → What It Taught Me

Chris almost never opens with a principle. He opens with a situation — specific, grounded, often named ("Tim," "Grant," "Queenstown"). Then he describes the cost or consequence. Then he draws the lesson.

**Example — Q6 (Mistake):**
> Situation: "Failing at the Paragliding World Championships."
> Cost: "It cost me a lot of time and a lot of money... it wasn't the outcome I wanted... it didn't give me the satisfaction I wanted."
> Lesson: "The cost taught me that chasing after an extrinsic goal doesn't necessarily bring me happiness."

**Example — Q5 (Risk):**
> Situation: "Buying out the paragliding school was definitely one of the scariest moments."
> Risk/cost: "The risk was that I would fail and lose everything."
> Outcome: "It was one of the best things I've ever done."

### How he builds credibility

Through lived consequence, not credentials. He never says "I have 20 years experience." He shows what experience cost him and what it produced. The credibility is implicit.

### How he handles complexity

He simplifies through analogy and sequence. He uses the "A leads to B leads to C" chain construction to walk through complexity:
> "They learn meteorology to a high level, and from that they can understand good judgement, and from good judgement they can make a clear decision of go or no-go."

He also uses before/after contrast to compress complexity:
> "I used to think success was... Now I think success is..."

### How he closes a thought

Direct summary or declarative statement. He doesn't leave things open. He lands the plane.
> "I believed that to be the right thing at the time and still do."
> "...and that creates longevity in the sport."
> "I knew I'd never go back there because I had found the bottom of the hole."

## SECTION 7: AUTHENTICITY MARKERS

### Communication quirks

1. **Names specific people and places without explaining them.** Tim. Grant. Queenstown. Wellington. Thailand. Spain. He doesn't set up characters — he drops you into the story.
   > "A specific client is Grant, who had learned at a couple of other schools in Thailand and Spain."

2. **Uses triadic lists to build intensity.** Not bullet points — a sentence that accumulates.
   > "It was very dark, very bleak, very lost, very alone."
   > "reset, regroup, and reimagine"

3. **Asks himself questions mid-answer and answers them.** He uses a rhetorical device of posing the next logical question before the reader can ask it.
   > "What happened on the other side of it? It was one of the best things I've ever done."
   > "Why does it keep happening? Because the schools take too much responsibility for their students."

4. **States the principle last, never first.** He earns the right to the principle through the story. He does not lead with the idea.

5. **Admits cost without self-pity.** He names what things cost him — time, money, friendship, certainty — and moves immediately to what that cost taught him. No dwelling.
   > "It cost me a lot of time and a lot of money... The cost taught me..."

6. **Uses "that" to signal conviction.** When he says "I believed that..." or "The cost taught me that..." it signals he has arrived at a conclusion through experience, not opinion.

7. **Never uses superlatives casually.** When he says "one of the best things I've ever done," it lands — because he hasn't said anything like it elsewhere.

### Perspective patterns

1. **Responsibility as freedom.** He consistently frames ownership — of outcomes, mistakes, decisions — as liberating rather than burdensome. This runs across Q9, Q10, Q11, Q15.

2. **The gap between external success and internal satisfaction.** He has lived and named this gap: chasing the World Championships, redefining success, losing Tim. He comes back to it repeatedly in different forms.

3. **Sequential competence over talent.** He doesn't believe in innate ability — he believes in finding what you care about and working. His origin story and his mother's principle both confirm this.

4. **Before/after as the unit of truth.** He structures proof through transformation — his own and his students'. The before and after is always specific, never abstract.

### Personal touches

- Names his deceased best friend: Tim
- Names his student: Grant
- References his mother directly, quotes her
- Anchors to specific ages: "18 to 23 years old"
- Anchors to specific places: Queenstown, Wellington, Thailand, Spain, New Zealand
- Uses a phrase that sounds spiritual without being religious: "dark night of the soul"

### Values that come through

- **Integrity:** "I believed that to be the right thing at the time and still do."
- **Self-reliance:** "If you want something, go out and get it."
- **Craftsmanship / doing it right:** Mother's quote, top student in NZ, buying and building the school properly.
- **Honesty about failure:** Named the World Champs failure directly. No euphemism.
- **Student autonomy over dependency:** The central philosophy of his teaching.

## SECTION 8: RED FLAGS — WHAT HE'D NEVER SAY

### Language he avoids

Chris never used corporate softeners, motivational filler, or vague positioning language across any of his 15 answers.

**Corporate jargon he never used:**
- "synergy," "leverage," "pivot," "ecosystem," "disruptive," "world-class," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class," "empower" (as a standalone buzzword), "value proposition," "holistic approach," "passion-driven," "game-changer"

**Overly formal constructions:**
- "One might argue..." / "It is important to note..." / "In conclusion..."
- Passive voice constructions: he is consistently the agent, not the subject of passive action

**Hype or exaggeration:**
- He never claimed to be the best, most experienced, or uniquely gifted
- He never promised outcomes without grounding them in process

**Tonal mismatches:**
- Motivational poster language: "Chase your dreams!" / "Nothing is impossible!"
- Self-deprecating humour to soften hard truths (he states hard truths plainly)
- Performed vulnerability ("I really struggled with this and it took everything I had...")

### Specific violations

✗ NEVER write: "I'm passionate about helping people reach their potential."
   Why: He never used the word "passionate" or "potential." He describes outcomes, not intentions.

✗ NEVER write: "We leverage a holistic approach to flight training."
   Why: He explicitly said in Q10 he doesn't do vague approach statements. "Be specific — I want to understand the mechanics, not just 'I take a holistic approach'" is how he frames what others get wrong.

✗ NEVER write: "Our world-class instructors..."
   Why: He does not use superlatives casually. When he does use them ("one of the best things I've ever done"), they are earned and singular.

✗ NEVER write: "It's been an incredible journey."
   Why: He never used the word "journey" or "incredible." He describes chapters and periods — not journeys.

✗ NEVER write: "I believe everyone has the potential to fly."
   Why: He believes in ownership and work, not potential. His framework is: want it → work for it → earn it.

✗ NEVER write: "Failure is just a stepping stone to success."
   Why: He is specific about what failure cost him. Generic platitudes about failure would read as dishonest given how concretely he named his own.

✗ NEVER write: "We work with you every step of the way."
   Why: His entire philosophy is the opposite — he trains pilots to not need the school. Dependency-language contradicts his core method.

✗ NEVER write: "Join our community of passionate pilots."
   Why: Neither "community" nor "passionate" appear in any of his answers. These are marketing words, not his words.

### Structural violations

- Never open a piece of content with a statistic or industry trend. He always opens with a person, a moment, or a situation.
- Never use bullet points as the primary structure. He thinks in chains and sequences, not lists.
- Never abstract the lesson before telling the story. His pattern is always: earn the principle with the experience first.
- Never use "you" as a command opener in the first sentence. He doesn't instruct from above — he demonstrates from inside.
