---

## ONE AVATAR: The Serious Aspirant

They've watched paragliders from the ground for months, maybe years. They follow pilots on Instagram, they've researched gear, they've looked up schools. The fantasy is clear: launching from a ridge, feeling the thermal lift beneath them, navigating cross-country on the power of the sun and the wind. But there's a gap — and they know it. They have the desire and none of the tools. They can't read weather. They don't know how to launch. They wouldn't know a stable day from a dangerous one. That gap isn't just practical. It sits in them as a kind of quiet frustration: wanting something deeply and not yet being capable of having it safely.

**The turning point trigger:**
A trip to Queenstown. A review that reads differently to the others. A recommendation from a pilot friend who trained at Infinity and came back changed. The moment they realise this isn't just about doing a course — it's about becoming someone who can fly.

**Their internal monologue:**
"I don't just want the licence. I want to actually know what I'm doing. I've seen what happens when people fly without proper training — or when a school just gets them through the course. I don't want that. I want someone to tell me the hard things and hold me to a real standard. If I'm going to do this, I'm doing it properly."

---

## ONE PROBLEM: THE VILLAIN

The problem is not the student. The villain is the industry standard — and the dangerous gap it leaves.

**External:** The student wants to fly independently, but without high-quality instruction, they graduate with a licence that doesn't match their actual capability. They're technically legal. They're not genuinely safe.

**Internal:** They feel the gap even if they can't name it. There's a kind of low-grade anxiety underneath every flight — a feeling that they're relying on luck as much as skill. They don't know what they don't know. That's the most dangerous place to be in aviation.

**Philosophical:** "Aspiring pilots shouldn't have to choose between getting access to the sport and getting the standard they actually need to fly for life."

**The Villain Statement:**
"The real problem in the Serious Aspirant's story is the low standard of paragliding instruction globally, which shows up as students leaving courses with licences but without genuine pilot judgement, makes them feel falsely ready and quietly uncertain, and represents the unfair truth that aviation is being sold like a holiday activity when it demands the respect of a discipline."

---

## ONE SOLUTION: 3-STEP PLAN

**Step 1 — Ground & Orient (Intro Day + PG1):**
Before any flight, the student handles the glider. They learn to launch it, control it, and stop it — on the ground, in real wind, with real consequence. This isn't orientation. It's the beginning of true responsibility. PG1 adds six short flights and ground school covering parachute deployment, landing approaches, radio, and logbook standards. By the end, they've already begun to feel what flying actually demands.

**Step 2 — Build the Pilot (PG2 · 34 flights):**
The core of the programme. Not just flight repetition — structured, high-standard instruction across three-axis glider control, descent techniques, advanced meteorology, decision-making processes, and go/no-go judgement. Chris and Jason hold every student to a level that goes far beyond the IPPI minimum. No student graduates PG2 because they hit 34 flights. They graduate because they've become pilots.

**Step 3 — Own the Sky (Autonomous Pilot):**
The graduate can assess conditions independently, choose their sites, make their go/no-go calls, and fly with confidence — not because they were told they're ready, but because they know it. This is the identity shift: from student to pilot. From someone who needs supervision to someone who carries the responsibility and capability to match.

## 7-PART BRAND STORY

### 1. A Character
Somewhere between watching paragliders soar above Queenstown and booking a lesson, there's a moment every Serious Aspirant has. They're standing at the bottom of a hill looking up, and they realise — they don't just want to try paragliding. They want to be able to fly. Really fly. Not once, not on a tandem with someone else at the controls, but alone. In their own time. On their own terms. The kind of flying that comes from knowing exactly what you're doing in the air.

### 2. Has a Problem
The problem is the gap. They can want it all they like, but wanting to be an autonomous pilot and having the judgement to actually be one are two completely different things. The world is full of paragliding schools that will issue a licence, count the flights, and send students off the hill calling them pilots. Most of those students will feel, quietly and honestly, that something is missing. That they're not quite sure what they'd do if conditions shifted. That they're leaning on luck in ways they can't fully account for. That's not freedom. That's exposure wearing a licence.

### 3. Meets a Guide
Chris Connolly has been flying for 15 years and teaching for 13 of those. He's trained at the best SIV school in the world, travels internationally every season to learn what equipment and technique advances mean for instruction, and has built Infinity Paragliding into the only paragliding school in New Zealand's Otago region — one that has consistently attracted international pilots who've already trained elsewhere and come looking for what their previous school couldn't give them. He knows what the gap looks like. And he knows how to close it.

### 4. Who Gives Them a Plan
The path is specific and it works. Intro Day: you meet the glider on the ground. You launch it, control it, stop it — with your own hands, in real conditions. PG1: six short flights and real ground school covering parachute deployment, landing approaches, radio procedure, and logbook. PG2: 34 flights, averaging five per day, building three-axis glider control, descent techniques, advanced meteorology, and the decision-making processes that separate pilots who know what they're doing from pilots who hope they do. The standard at every stage goes beyond what IPPI requires, because what IPPI requires is the floor — not the ceiling.

### 5. And Calls Them to Action
**Direct CTA:** Book your Intro Day and begin building the pilot you actually want to be.
**Transitional CTA:** Start with an instructional tandem — fly with Chris or Jason and feel what high-standard instruction looks like from the inside.

### 6. That Helps Them Avoid Failure
The alternative is well-documented. Pilots who trained to the minimum standard find themselves standing on hills they're not sure they should be launching from. They make go/no-go calls based on hope rather than knowledge. They put themselves in situations they can't read, in conditions they can't assess, with decision-making tools they were never given. Some of them will be fine. Some won't. Aviation doesn't grade on a curve. The standard you leave training with is the standard you'll fly with for the rest of your flying life.

### 7. And Ends in Success
This is Grant's story. He'd trained in Thailand and Spain. Two schools, two countries, two licences worth of instruction — and when he arrived at Infinity, he sat down with Chris and realised how much he didn't know. He stayed for a month. He did 45 flights. At flight 25, something shifted. His alpine background — the risk management, the judgement calls, the habit of treating the environment seriously — combined with genuinely high-level instruction to produce a pilot of real capability. He wasn't handed a certificate. He earned a transformation. That's what it looks like when someone trains with Infinity Paragliding.
